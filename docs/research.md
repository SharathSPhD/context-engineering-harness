# Context as a first-class cognitive primitive: three traditions converge

**The central claim of this synthesis is that context engineering in LLMs has independently rediscovered architectural principles that cognitive neuroscience has characterized empirically and that classical Indian epistemology formalized two millennia ago.** The three traditions differ radically in their tools (benchmarks, fMRI, *pramāṇa*-śāstra) but converge on a small set of recurring structures: a bounded active buffer operating under attentional selection; a tiered memory hierarchy with principled forgetting; generative priors that bias interpretation; and explicit machinery for resolving conflict between sources of information. The convergence is not metaphorical — it is predictive. When the same solution appears under three independent selection pressures (engineering utility, biological adaptation, philosophical scrutiny), that is prima facie evidence the solution is structural rather than incidental, and tells us where LLM systems are still missing components that both brains and classical analyses consider load-bearing. This document lays out the three domains in compressed technical form, builds a formal conceptual mapping, identifies where all three diagnose the same unsolved problems, proposes seven falsifiable research hypotheses that operate across the bridge, and flags five frontier directions unlikely to emerge from ML research alone.

## Domain 1: The mechanics and pathologies of the LLM context window

**The transformer context window is a fixed-length, permutation-invariant buffer with O(L²) attention cost and O(L) KV-cache memory**, whose utilization is non-uniform and increasingly pathological with length. Vaswani et al. (2017) defined the substrate: scaled dot-product self-attention softmax(QKᵀ/√d_k)V, with the KV cache (2·n·h·d_h·L) storing all prior token projections. Positional information is injected externally — **RoPE** (Su et al. 2021) rotates Q/K pairs by position-dependent angles so that attention depends only on relative position, while **ALiBi** (Press, Smith, and Lewis 2021) adds a linear distance penalty to attention logits. RoPE fails to extrapolate past its maximum trained wavelength; ALiBi extrapolates but bakes in a monotonic recency bias. Efficient implementations (FlashAttention, Dao 2022–2024; PagedAttention, Kwon et al. 2023) have shrunk constants but not the complexity class.

**Empirical utilization of the nominal context window is severely non-uniform.** Liu et al. (2023, "Lost in the Middle") showed a U-shaped accuracy curve across GPT-3.5, Claude, and MPT when a gold passage is placed at varying positions in a multi-document prompt — strong primacy and recency, weak middle. The Chroma "Context Rot" report (Hong, Troynikov, and Huber 2025) extended this across 18 frontier models (GPT-4.1, Claude 4, Gemini 2.5, Qwen3), demonstrating that **performance degrades non-uniformly with length even on trivial copy tasks**, and that semantic similarity between needle and haystack, distractor presence, and haystack coherence all modulate degradation independently. Hsieh et al.'s **RULER** benchmark (2024) found that of 17 models claiming ≥32K context, roughly half failed to maintain quality at nominal length. Karpinska et al.'s **NoCha** (2024) cleanly separated sentence-level retrieval (easy) from global narrative reasoning (hard). **HELMET** (Yen et al. 2024) showed needle-in-a-haystack does not correlate with downstream application tasks. The cumulative picture: effective context is substantially shorter than nominal context, and *how* information is presented matters more than *whether* it is present.

**The solutions landscape decomposes into retrieval, compression, external memory, alternative architectures, and positional training.** Retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al. 2020) now includes **HyDE** (Gao et al. 2023) which embeds a hypothetical answer rather than the query, **RAPTOR** (Sarthi et al. 2024) which builds a recursive summary tree, **GraphRAG** (Edge et al. 2024) which builds an entity-relation graph with community summaries, and **Self-RAG** (Asai et al. 2023) which fine-tunes the model to emit retrieval-control tokens. Compression work includes **LLMLingua/LongLLMLingua** (Jiang et al. 2023–2024) for information-theoretic token pruning, and **AutoCompressors** (Chevalier et al. 2023) for trainable summary vectors. Memory architectures include **MemGPT/Letta** (Packer et al. 2023), which casts the context window as RAM and external stores as disk, and **CoALA** (Sumers et al. 2024), whose SOAR-inspired taxonomy of working, episodic, semantic, and procedural memory underpins most serious agent designs. Efficient-attention lineages culminate in state-space hybrids — **Mamba** (Gu and Dao 2023) and **Jamba** (Lieber et al. 2024) — that preserve a compressed recurrent state. Long-context training extends RoPE via Position Interpolation (Chen et al. 2023), **YaRN** (Peng et al. 2023), and LongRoPE (Ding et al. 2024), the last reaching 2M+ tokens. Prompt caching (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google) amortizes long fixed prefixes across requests.

**Context engineering as a named discipline crystallized mid-2025.** Tobi Lütke (June 18, 2025) and Andrej Karpathy (June 25, 2025) advocated the term over "prompt engineering"; Harrison Chase of LangChain operationalized it as "building dynamic systems to provide the right information and tools in the right format." Two canonical essays anchor the field: Walden Yan's **"Don't Build Multi-Agents"** (Cognition, June 12, 2025), whose twin principles are "share context, and share full agent traces, not just individual messages" and "actions carry implicit decisions, and conflicting decisions carry bad results"; and Anthropic's **"Effective Context Engineering for AI Agents"** (September 29, 2025), which frames context as a finite "attention budget" and prescribes compaction, external note-taking, and scoped sub-agents. The Cognition-vs.-Anthropic disagreement is the field's productive tension. Production systems (Claude Code, Cursor, Devin) have converged on **compaction at thresholds, file-based memory (CLAUDE.md, plan.md), and tool-mediated just-in-time retrieval** rather than up-front context stuffing.

## Domain 2: How biological cognition actually manages context

**The brain solves context through a coordinated hierarchy operating on timescales from milliseconds to years**, with no single structure playing the role of "the context window" — a fact that is itself diagnostic for AI design. Working memory, as formalized by Baddeley and Hitch (1974) and extended with the **episodic buffer** (Baddeley 2000), provides the active buffer: a limited-capacity store that binds multimodal content into coherent scenes. Capacity estimates settle on **~4 chunks** (Cowan 2001) in the focus of attention, and Miller's classical 7±2 (1956) post-chunking span. The buffer is neurally realized in persistent delay-period firing in dlPFC and posterior parietal cortex (Funahashi, Bruce, and Goldman-Rakic 1989), formalized as attractor dynamics in balanced recurrent networks (Compte et al. 2000; Wang 2001), and supplemented by **activity-silent** synaptic traces (Stokes 2015; Lundqvist et al. 2016). Miller and Cohen's (2001) integrative theory makes PFC the source of top-down contextual bias that configures posterior systems for task demands.

**Memory systems form a structured background** against which current context is interpreted. Tulving's (1972, 1985) distinction between episodic (autonoetic, hippocampus-dependent) and semantic memory, Squire's (1992) broader declarative/nondeclarative division, and the identification of procedural memory with basal ganglia (Graybiel 2008) partition long-term storage by representational format and encoding dynamics. Schemas (Bartlett 1932; Ghosh and Gilboa 2014) are the highest level: associative, adaptable, hierarchical knowledge structures. Van Kesteren et al.'s **SLIMM** (2012) framework shows that when new information is *congruent* with existing schemas, vmPFC enables rapid neocortical integration while inhibiting the hippocampus; when *incongruent*, hippocampal pattern-separated encoding takes over. This bifurcation is directly relevant to AI: it separates "fits the prior" from "must be remembered verbatim."

**Attention selects and weights context** through a combination of early/late filtering (Broadbent 1958; Treisman 1964), the cocktail-party effect (Cherry 1953), and dorsal/ventral attention networks (Corbetta and Shulman 2002) that implement goal-directed selection and salience-driven interrupts respectively. Desimone and Duncan's (1995) **biased competition** model unifies these: sensory representations compete, top-down PFC signals encoding the WM template bias the competition, and the winner gains access to downstream systems. Attention, mechanistically, is gain modulation (Reynolds and Heeger 2009) of relevance.

**Consolidation and forgetting are active, adaptive processes.** Hippocampal sharp-wave ripples (Buzsáki 2015) compress waking experience into replay events during quiet wake and non-REM sleep (Wilson and McNaughton 1994); replay is prioritized by reward (Ambrose, Pfeiffer, and Foster 2016) and coupled to cortical slow oscillations and spindles (Diekelmann and Born 2010). **Complementary Learning Systems** theory (McClelland, McNaughton, and O'Reilly 1995; updated in Kumaran, Hassabis, and McClelland 2016) explains the division of labor: hippocampus learns fast and sparsely, neocortex learns slow and distributedly via interleaved replay, avoiding catastrophic interference. Richards and Frankland's (2017) "Persistence and Transience" paper reframes forgetting as adaptive: **neurogenesis-driven clearance, AMPA-receptor removal, and synaptic scaling** optimize generalization and decision-making by pruning outdated and overfit traces.

**Predictive coding makes context generative, not just reactive.** Helmholtz's unconscious inference, formalized by Rao and Ballard (1999) and generalized in Friston's (2010) free-energy principle, treats perception and cognition as hierarchical Bayesian inference: top-down predictions meet bottom-up evidence, only prediction errors propagate upward, and precision (expected reliability) gates how strongly errors update beliefs. Feldman and Friston (2010) identify precision-weighting with attentional gain, unifying attention and prediction under a single contextual account.

**Language comprehension is the paradigm test-bed.** Kintsch's (1988) construction-integration model stages comprehension as build-everything-then-settle-by-coherence. Zwaan and Radvansky's (1998) **situation models** have five updated dimensions — space, time, causation, intentionality, protagonist — each shift incurring costs. Zacks et al.'s (2007) event segmentation theory ties boundaries to prediction failure; Baldassano et al. (2017) show nested cortical event structure matching behavioral segmentation. Clark's (1977) bridging inferences and Gibson's (1998) dependency locality quantify how memory and schema interact with incoming linguistic context.

## Domain 3: The Vedic engineering of knowing

**Classical Indian philosophy treated knowing as an engineered process with explicit components, fallibility conditions, and repair procedures.** Three schools are directly relevant. **Nyāya** (Akṣapāda Gautama, c. 200 CE; Navya-Nyāya from Gaṅgeśa c. 1325) is the logic-and-epistemology school. Its four *pramāṇa*s — **pratyakṣa** (perception), **anumāna** (inference), **upamāna** (comparison), and **śabda** (testimony) — are not categories of belief but operational instruments, each with defined success conditions and failure modes (*hetvābhāsa*, fallacies of reason: *savyabhicāra*, *viruddha*, *satpratipakṣa*, *asiddha*, *bādhita*). **Pratyakṣa** is staged: *nirvikalpaka* (non-conceptual, pre-categorial) followed by *savikalpaka* (categorial, structured by qualifier-qualificand relations). Inference is five-limbed (*pratijñā, hetu, udāharaṇa, upanaya, nigamana*) and turns on **vyāpti** — invariable concomitance between the probans (*hetu*) and the probandum (*sādhya*) — defeated by an *upādhi*, a latent condition that accompanies the sādhya without always accompanying the hetu. **Tarka** is hypothetical reasoning used to defend *vyāpti* claims against skeptical counterexample. Navya-Nyāya (Gaṅgeśa's *Tattvacintāmaṇi*) added a precise technical notation with **avacchedaka** (delimitor — the exact property under which a relation obtains), **viśeṣya/viśeṣaṇa/saṃsarga** (qualificand/qualifier/relation), and treatments of context-sensitive meaning that anticipate modern formal semantics.

**Vaiśeṣika** (Kaṇāda, possibly 2nd century BCE; systematized by Praśastapāda c. 4th–6th century CE) is the ontology-and-categorisation school. Its seven **padārtha**s — *dravya* (substance, 9 kinds), *guṇa* (quality, 24 kinds), *karma* (action), *sāmānya* (universal), *viśeṣa* (ultimate particular/differentiator), *samavāya* (inherence), and *abhāva* (absence) — provide a type system for reality. **Sāmānya and viśeṣa together solve the particular-universal problem**: universals inhere in particulars via *samavāya*, and *viśeṣa* distinguishes otherwise-identical eternal atoms. Vaiśeṣika's **asatkāryavāda** (effect newly produced from cause, not pre-existent) and its **atomism** (paramāṇu → dvyaṇuka → tryaṇuka) provide a principled account of how discrete primitives compose into novel macroscopic structures. The Naiyāyika-Vaiśeṣika alliance treats *manas* as the inner sense that mediates sequential perception and is itself atomic (monadic, single-channel), structurally enforcing serial processing.

**Advaita Vedānta** (Śaṅkara, c. 700–750 CE; systematized by Dharmarāja's *Vedānta-paribhāṣā*, 17th c.) is the school of non-dual consciousness. It distinguishes four components of the inner instrument, the **antaḥkaraṇa**: *manas* (indecisive, sensory), *buddhi* (discriminative, decisive), *citta* (memory/reflection), and *ahaṃkāra* (ego/I-maker). Its theory of perception uses **vṛtti** (mental modification): the antaḥkaraṇa *assumes the form* of its object, with **vṛtti-vyāpti** (mental pervasion, required for all cognition) distinguished from **phala-vyāpti** (consciousness-as-result, not required for self-cognition). The **pañca-kośa** (five sheaths) — annamaya, prāṇamaya, manomaya, vijñānamaya, ānandamaya — stratify experience by level of abstraction. **Māyā** and **avidyā** generate **adhyāsa** (superimposition): contextual misassignment, where an attribute from one locus is erroneously attributed to another (the canonical rope-snake, or the conflation of ātman and anātman). **Bādha** (sublation) is the repair operation — when a higher-order cognition cancels a lower one (the snake "sublated" by seeing the rope). **Viveka** is the discriminative faculty that operationalizes sublation. **Saṃskāra**s and **vāsanā**s are latent impressions stored in citta that condition future cognition — the tradition's explicit account of priors.

**Cross-cuttingly, all three schools take context seriously as a load-bearing philosophical notion.** The debate between **pramāṇa-samplava** (multiple pramāṇas can target the same object; Nyāya) and **pramāṇa-vyavasthā** (each pramāṇa has an exclusive object-sphere; Buddhist epistemologists after Dignāga) is a direct debate about whether information sources fuse or stay segregated. **Svataḥ-prāmāṇya** (intrinsic validity of cognition until defeated; Mīmāṃsā, Vedānta) versus **parataḥ-prāmāṇya** (validity requires external corroboration; Nyāya) is a debate about trust models for internal computation. The six khyātivādas (theories of error) systematically enumerate failure modes of perception — *anyathākhyāti* (Nyāya: grasping something as it is not), *viparītakhyāti* (Bhāṭṭa: inverted identification), *anirvacanīyakhyāti* (Advaita: the illusory object is inexplicably neither real nor unreal). This is an error taxonomy of a sophistication that no current LLM hallucination ontology matches.

## A formal conceptual mapping

The three vocabularies map onto one another with surprising precision, but the mapping is not one-to-one — each tradition partitions the problem space slightly differently, and the residuals are where novel research questions live.

| LLM context engineering | Cognitive neuroscience | Vedic epistemology |
|---|---|---|
| **Context window / KV cache** | Episodic buffer + WM persistent activity (dlPFC) | *Antaḥkaraṇa* operating as single *manas*-gated buffer |
| **System prompt / persona** | Task set maintained in dlPFC (Miller & Cohen 2001) | *Saṃskāra*/*vāsanā* — latent dispositions conditioning vṛtti |
| **Retrieval-Augmented Generation** | Hippocampal episodic retrieval cued by current state | *Smṛti* as derived cognition, invoked by *pratyabhijñā* (recognition) |
| **Attention softmax weighting** | Biased competition (Desimone & Duncan 1995); precision-weighted prediction error | *Pratyakṣa* with *avacchedaka*-delimited salience |
| **Positional encoding (RoPE/ALiBi)** | Temporal context model; event-segmentation boundaries | Sequential *manas* — the serial, single-object constraint |
| **Context compression / summarization** | Schema-based chunking; SLIMM-mediated neocortical integration | **Sāmānya-Viśeṣa**: subsuming particulars under universals while preserving individuating marks |
| **Prefix/prompt caching** | Consolidated semantic/procedural memory; automaticity | *Vāsanā* as sedimented prior-cognition, cheaply re-activated |
| **Multi-agent context handoff** | Inter-regional communication via prefrontal broadcast; common ground (Clark 1996) | *Vyāpti*-governed inference chains; *parārthānumāna* (inference for others) |
| **Forgetting / pruning / compaction** | Adaptive transience (Richards & Frankland 2017); neurogenesis clearance | *Bādha* (sublation) and *viveka*-driven removal of *adhyāsa* |
| **Hallucination / context rot** | Confabulation under weak priors; schema intrusion | *Adhyāsa* (superimposition); *anyathākhyāti* / *viparītakhyāti* |
| **Benchmarks (RULER, NoCha)** | Dependency-locality costs (Gibson); event-boundary recall | *Hetvābhāsa* taxonomy — catalogued inferential failures |
| **Self-RAG / reflection tokens** | Metacognitive monitoring; anterior cingulate conflict detection | *Anuvyavasāya* (second-order cognition certifying the first) |
| **Agent memory (MemGPT/Letta)** | Complementary Learning Systems (McClelland et al. 1995) | Four-fold *antaḥkaraṇa*: *manas*–*buddhi*–*citta*–*ahaṃkāra* as explicitly separated stores |
| **Architectural decisions preserved in CLAUDE.md** | Schemas as mPFC-anchored stable priors | *Siddhānta* — established conclusions that structure further inquiry |

Three structural observations fall out of the mapping. **First, the LLM community has no analogue of the** *buddhi*/*manas* **distinction** — the separation between indecisive, sensory-bound mental activity and decisive, discriminative judgment is not cleanly reflected in the flat "message buffer + tool calls" of modern agent frameworks. The closest cousin is Self-RAG's reflection tokens, but these are shallow. **Second, neuroscience has no analogue of** *avacchedaka* **at representational resolution** — the brain does not, to our knowledge, maintain explicit limitor properties with the compositional precision of Navya-Nyāya, which means computational implementations of *avacchedaka*-semantics may be a genuine new primitive rather than a re-derivation. **Third, the Vedic tradition has no analogue of quadratic attention cost** — its concept of *manas* as monadic and serial *enforces* a bandwidth limit by design, whereas transformer attention imposes one as an unintended consequence. The three traditions agree a bandwidth limit is necessary but differ on whether it should be a designed constraint (Vedic), an emergent capacity ceiling (neuroscience), or a cost to minimize (LLM).

## Core unsolved problems, read through all three lenses

**Problem 1: Effective context is a small fraction of nominal context, and we have no mechanistic theory of why.** Lost-in-the-middle (Liu et al. 2023), context rot (Hong et al. 2025), and RULER (Hsieh et al. 2024) document the phenomenon. Cognitive science names the same pattern in humans — primacy/recency in serial recall, event-boundary forgetting (Zacks et al. 2007), dependency locality cost (Gibson 1998) — and attributes it to capacity limits, attractor interference, and schema-mediated chunking. Advaita treats it as *āvaraṇa* (concealing power of *māyā*): information *within* the buffer is nonetheless functionally absent because it is not currently illuminated by *vṛtti-vyāpti*. All three suggest the problem is not "add more tokens" but "align active illumination with task-relevance."

**Problem 2: No principled, automatic policy for what to forget.** CoALA taxonomies and Letta-style tiered memories describe storage; *when* to compress, merge, or delete remains heuristic. Neuroscience shows forgetting is actively engineered — neurogenesis, synaptic scaling, sleep-gated replay prioritization (Richards & Frankland 2017; Kumaran et al. 2016). Advaita demands *viveka* (discrimination) as a distinct cognitive faculty; Navya-Nyāya provides *upādhi*-detection as a formal mechanism for invalidating previously-trusted inferences. Current LLM compaction lacks both the graded decay schedule and the formal conflict-detection.

**Problem 3: Multi-agent context sharing has no theory.** The Cognition/Anthropic disagreement (June–September 2025) is empirically productive but theoretically unresolved. Neuroscience provides common-ground theory (Clark 1996), inter-regional broadcast, and the role of shared schema in cooperation. Nyāya's *parārthānumāna* — inference articulated *for another* — is explicitly a different construct from *svārthānumāna* (inference for oneself), requiring the full five-membered syllogistic form precisely because the shared context cannot be presumed. **LLM multi-agent protocols currently resemble svārthānumāna pretending to be parārthānumāna**: the compressed trace is passed without the redundancy that makes external reconstruction possible.

**Problem 4: Evaluation does not predict deployment.** HELMET (Yen et al. 2024) showed NIAH does not correlate with real tasks. Cognitive science's situation-model evaluation (Zwaan & Radvansky 1998) evaluates multi-dimensional coherence, not retrieval. Nyāya evaluates inference at the level of complete, defended *vyāpti*, including active search for *upādhi* counterexamples. Current LLM evaluation lacks both multi-dimensional coherence metrics and adversarial counterexample search.

**Problem 5: We have no model of how priors, evidence, and precision compose.** Predictive coding says context *is* the prior, weighted against evidence by precision (Friston 2010). Nyāya *pramāṇa-samplava* vs *vyavasthā* is the same debate: do multiple sources fuse or dominate? LLMs mostly implement late-concatenation ("here are the retrieved docs, answer the question") with no principled precision weighting. RAG re-rankers are a crude precision proxy, but fail to handle conflicting sources — a problem both Advaita's *bādha* and Bayesian updating solve in principled ways.

**Problem 6: Hallucination taxonomies are impoverished.** LLMs typically distinguish "factual" vs. "non-factual" hallucination. The six khyātivādas provide a vastly richer typology: *asatkhyāti* (hallucinating pure non-being), *ātmakhyāti* (projecting internal content as external), *akhyāti* (failing to notice difference — two true cognitions combined wrongly), *anyathākhyāti* (misidentifying one real thing as another real thing), *viparītakhyāti* (inverted identification), *anirvacanīyakhyāti* (a novel confabulation). Each predicts different failure signatures and different corrections. **No LLM safety system currently labels hallucinations by type with this granularity**, and yet it is straightforward to define training signals and evaluation sets for each.

**Problem 7: Context engineering remains artisanal.** Anthropic's "Effective Context Engineering" guide (September 2025) is prescriptive but not causal — no automated policy follows from it. Neuroscience knows that PFC rule-maintenance is hierarchically organized (Koechlin & Summerfield 2007). Navya-Nyāya gives an explicit algebra (*avacchedaka*, *nirūpaka*, *paryāpti*) for formalizing which context is currently binding. A formal context algebra — analogous to Navya-Nyāya's notation — may be a prerequisite to automating context policies.

## Seven research hypotheses bridging the three domains

### H1: The "context rot" curve can be predicted by schema-congruence, not length

**Claim.** The empirically observed non-uniform degradation with input length (Chroma 2025) is explained better by the ratio of schema-congruent to schema-incongruent tokens in the input than by raw length. Specifically, performance should degrade when distractors share surface features with the target but differ semantically — the regime where human schemas also fail (van Kesteren et al. 2012; SLIMM).

**Predicts.** Two contexts of equal token length but different schema-congruence distributions will produce different error rates, and the direction is predictable: high-congruence distractors hurt more than random distractors.

**Test.** Construct paired long-context prompts at 32K/64K/128K tokens. Version A: distractors drawn from same topical domain with overlapping surface features (congruent). Version B: distractors drawn from random unrelated topics. Evaluate on frontier models (Claude, GPT, Gemini, Qwen3, Jamba). Compare error rates against a baseline that controls only for length. Benchmarks: RULER extended with congruence-controlled distractors; NoCha for narrative congruence.

### H2: Precision-weighted retrieval outperforms top-k retrieval

**Claim.** Current RAG systems treat retrieved documents as equally trustworthy after re-ranking. A Bayesian scheme that assigns explicit precision (expected reliability) to each source and propagates precision through generation — analogous to Friston's (2010) precision-weighted prediction error and Nyāya's *pramāṇa-samplava* — will outperform top-k on tasks involving conflicting sources.

**Predicts.** On tasks where 2–3 retrieved documents give contradictory answers, a precision-weighted system resolves correctly more often and produces explicit uncertainty markers aligned with source disagreement. Calibration (ECE) improves measurably.

**Test.** Build a "conflicting-sources QA" benchmark: questions where retrieved corpora contain both correct and incorrect answers. Compare (a) vanilla RAG, (b) Self-RAG, (c) a Bayesian variant that elicits per-source precision scores (from metadata, recency, author authority, internal consistency) and weights in the generation step. Measure accuracy, calibration, and a new metric for "correctly flagged conflict."

### H3: A buddhi/manas two-stage agent architecture reduces context rot

**Claim.** An agent architecture that explicitly separates a fast, broad-attention *manas*-module (drafts, surfaces candidates, does not commit) from a slow, narrow *buddhi*-module (discriminates, commits, sublates) outperforms single-stage Self-RAG-style architectures on long-context reasoning tasks — because it mirrors the biological construction-integration pattern (Kintsch 1988) and Advaita's vṛtti-then-niścaya staging.

**Predicts.** The two-stage system shows lower hallucination rate on NoCha-style global comprehension, higher accuracy on multi-hop RULER tasks, and — critically — better performance on tasks that require *withholding* a confident answer when evidence is weak.

**Test.** Implement the two-stage agent using the same base model for both stages (to isolate the architectural contribution). Benchmarks: HELMET (application-centric), NoCha (narrative), a new "withhold-or-answer" benchmark with calibrated ambiguous inputs. Compare against Self-RAG, CRAG, and single-call baselines.

### H4: Explicit event-boundary detection improves agent long-session performance

**Claim.** Long agent sessions (Claude Code, Devin) currently compact on fixed token thresholds. Replacing threshold-triggered compaction with **event-boundary-triggered compaction** — detecting moments when the current task's predictions fail, matching Zacks et al.'s (2007) event segmentation — will yield higher session-success rates at equal token budget.

**Predicts.** Sessions that compact at prediction-failure events preserve more task-relevant state per token, and recover better after compaction because the compaction happens at natural cognitive boundaries rather than arbitrary positions.

**Test.** Instrument Claude Code or an open equivalent (e.g., Aider, OpenHands) to detect boundaries via: (a) sudden increase in per-token surprise, (b) task-switch signals, (c) prediction-failure signatures. Compare against token-threshold compaction on SWE-bench Verified, long-horizon Aider benchmarks, and synthetic multi-task suites. Metric: task success rate at equal total token budget.

### H5: A navya-nyāya-style avacchedaka notation can serve as a formal context algebra

**Claim.** A lightweight formal annotation language — inspired by Navya-Nyāya's *avacchedaka* (limitor), *viśeṣya/viśeṣaṇa* (qualificand/qualifier), and *pratiyogin* (counter-positive of absence) — injected into system prompts or tool-call protocols, will yield measurable improvements in multi-agent coordination by making context sharing explicit rather than implicit.

**Predicts.** Multi-agent systems where messages are annotated with limitors ("this fact holds *under the condition that* ...") produce fewer conflicting actions and fewer wasted tool calls. Improvement should be largest in Cognition-style coordination failures (parallel subagents making incompatible decisions).

**Test.** Build a benchmark of coordination-failure tasks (parallel code refactors, shared-state edits, the Flappy Bird art-style scenario Yan cites). Evaluate four conditions: (a) no annotation; (b) natural-language caveats; (c) structured JSON metadata; (d) an explicit limitor/qualifier notation. Measure task completion rate and inter-agent conflict rate.

### H6: Classification of hallucinations by khyātivāda type enables targeted mitigation

**Claim.** Different hallucination types (mapped onto the six khyātivādas) have different root causes and respond to different interventions. Specifically, *anyathākhyāti*-type hallucinations (misidentifying one real entity as another) respond to retrieval; *ātmakhyāti*-type (projecting internal pattern as external fact) respond to uncertainty calibration training; *anirvacanīyakhyāti*-type (novel confabulation) require generation-time constraints.

**Predicts.** A diagnostic classifier trained to label hallucinations by khyātivāda type will show that targeted interventions (retrieval vs. calibration vs. constrained decoding) reduce their respective types disproportionately, while a single "fix hallucinations" intervention shows diluted effects across all types.

**Test.** Annotate a subset of HaluEval, TruthfulQA, and a narrative benchmark (FACTS grounding suite, Anthropic 2024) by khyātivāda type. Train a lightweight classifier. Evaluate three targeted mitigation pipelines (retrieval-heavy, calibration-heavy, constrained-decoding-heavy) and show they disproportionately reduce their target type. This builds a falsifiable diagnostic hallucination ontology.

### H7: Adaptive forgetting schedules derived from hippocampal replay statistics outperform fixed compaction

**Claim.** LLM agent memory currently uses either no forgetting (pure append) or fixed summarization. A schedule inspired by hippocampal replay prioritization (reward-weighted, recency-weighted, conflict-weighted; Ambrose et al. 2016) and adult neurogenesis clearance dynamics (Richards & Frankland 2017) will outperform fixed schedules on long-horizon tasks requiring both stability and adaptation.

**Predicts.** Agents with biologically-calibrated forgetting show better performance on environments that include *distribution shift* — where outdated context actively harms, and clearance is adaptive. They should match or slightly underperform on stationary tasks.

**Test.** Build a long-horizon agent benchmark with controlled distribution shifts (e.g., a codebase that changes API conventions midstream; a dialogue where user preferences reverse). Compare: (a) no forgetting, (b) fixed compaction, (c) recency-weighted forgetting, (d) reward-weighted replay-style forgetting, (e) a hybrid that clears provably-contradicted memory first (a computational analogue of *bādha*). Metric: performance on post-shift tasks relative to pre-shift tasks.

## Frontier directions from the neuroscience–Vedic cross-pollination

Several directions emerge from the cross-domain reading that are not presently mainstream in ML research, and that are unlikely to emerge from within ML alone because they depend on primitives the field has not yet named.

**Witness-consciousness architectures.** Advaita's *sākṣī* is context-invariant awareness: the stable perspective against which *vṛtti*s arise and pass. Agents today lack any analogue — the "persona" is a token sequence, not a stable reference frame. A concrete operationalization would be a frozen high-level summary that is (a) visible to every sub-process, (b) never itself rewritten by context operations, (c) serves as the reference against which all compactions are judged for identity preservation. This is stronger than system-prompt persistence and weaker than fine-tuning; it occupies an architectural slot the field has not yet named. It would be testable against agent-identity-drift benchmarks.

**Formalized sublation (bādha) as a native operation.** When new information contradicts stored memory, current agents either ignore the conflict or heuristically merge. Advaita's *bādha* operation is explicit: the later, stronger cognition *cancels* the earlier in the same locus without erasing its trace. Implementing bādha as a typed memory operation — with timestamps, provenance, and explicit reference to the sublated content — would provide auditable reasoning about belief revision that neither traditional databases nor vector stores currently supply. This connects to the AI safety problem of provable belief updates.

**Kośa-style layered representation.** The pañca-kośa's five-sheath model (from gross physical to subtle-conceptual to bliss/reward) suggests that representations at different levels of abstraction should be separately addressable and separately consolidated. Current LLMs collapse all abstraction levels into a single token stream. A multi-stream architecture with explicit sheath-like layers, each with its own buffer and consolidation rule, would be testable against tasks requiring multi-scale reasoning (e.g., proof generation, long-form code that must maintain both low-level syntax and high-level architecture).

**Asatkāryavāda as generative compositionality.** Vaiśeṣika's insistence that effects are *newly produced* from combinations of causes — against Sāṃkhya's *satkāryavāda* — yields a principled atomistic compositionality. For LLMs, this translates to a research program on whether truly novel combinations can be generated from bounded primitive vocabularies under explicit composition rules, rather than interpolated from training data. This reframes creativity in LLMs as an engineering question about combinatorial closure.

**Svataḥ-prāmāṇya trust models for self-verifying agents.** The Mīmāṃsā/Advaita position that cognitions are *innocent until defeated* is a tractable trust model: treat model outputs as presumptively valid, allocate compute to defeating them only under specific triggers (contradiction, surprise, high-stakes). Compare against the dominant current posture of pervasive self-doubt (chain-of-thought, verify-before-trust, constitutional critique). The trade-off is empirically open and worth systematic study on tasks with varying stakes and latency constraints. A formal result would identify regimes where svataḥ-prāmāṇya dominates parataḥ-prāmāṇya and vice versa.

## Conclusion: context as engineered alignment, not engineered storage

The three traditions' convergence points to a single reconceptualization: **context is not a container but an alignment**. The LLM context window, the episodic buffer, and the *antaḥkaraṇa* are not primarily storage devices; they are sites where incoming information is aligned with standing priors under bounded attention, such that downstream action is configured correctly. When the alignment fails — through dilution, interference, schema mismatch, or *adhyāsa* — performance collapses regardless of nominal capacity. Every successful intervention the LLM field has produced (compaction, prefix caching, Self-RAG, biological-looking tiered memory) is an alignment intervention in disguise. **The engineering target is not bigger buffers but better alignment**, and the missing primitives — explicit limitors, typed sublation, witness-invariant references, schema-congruence-aware compression, precision-weighted multi-source fusion, khyātivāda-typed error handling — are exactly those that neuroscience and classical Indian philosophy named long ago. The practical upshot for researchers building agent systems in 2026 is that further scaling of the context window, absent these primitives, is expected to yield diminishing returns; and the most productive research frontier is the one that treats each of the hypotheses above as falsifiable engineering claims rather than speculative analogies. The synthesis is not that ancient traditions "predicted" modern AI — it is that three independent investigations of how minded systems handle information converged on the same small set of load-bearing structures, and the LLM field can move faster by importing the structures it is currently in the process of rediscovering by trial.
